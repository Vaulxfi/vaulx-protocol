VAULX WHITE PAPER — MODULE 5: BUSINESS MODEL AND MOAT
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Vaulx operates a two-sided crowdlending marketplace secured by physical luxury collateral. On one side, lenders deposit stablecoins into pooled vaults and earn yield. On the other side, asset-rich borrowers pledge physical luxury goods — watches, jewelry, art, vehicles — to receive stablecoin loans. Vaulx earns revenue from origination fees, interest spread, rollover fees, and secondary platform services. The business model is structurally profitable at modest loan volume, scales with the Brazilian luxury asset base, and defends itself through a custody moat that no pure-technology competitor can replicate in under 18 months.

SECTION 1: PLATFORM ARCHITECTURE — HOW THE TWO-SIDED MARKET WORKS
1.1 The Lender Side: Vaults
Lenders deposit USDC or BRLz (Brazilian stablecoin) into Vaulx vaults — smart contract-managed liquidity pools. Each vault is designated to a specific loan duration bucket (30-day, 60-day, 90-day). Upon deposit, the lender receives a vault share token (vGFI) representing their proportional claim on the pool's total assets plus accrued interest. This follows the ERC-4626 / SPL Token vault share standard, adapted for Solana.

The vault share token is the critical accounting unit. As loans are repaid and interest accumulates, the vault's NAV increases, and each vGFI token appreciates relative to the underlying stablecoin. Lenders earn yield passively — no active management required. Yield is generated by the spread between what Vaulx charges borrowers and what it pays lenders.

Target lender yield: 8–14% APY in USDC terms, competitive with comparable RWA crowdlending protocols. Maple Finance's Blue Chip Secured product delivered 10.2% net APY in 2024; its High Yield Secured delivered 16.83% net APY, both without token incentives. Vaulx's physical-collateral product targets the 10–14% range for base vaults, with higher-yield tranches for backers accepting junior-pool exposure.

1.2 The Borrower Side: Collateralized Loans
Borrowers submit a physical asset through Vaulx's intake process (described in Module 3). Upon verified custody and cNFT minting, the smart contract releases USDC or BRLz directly to the borrower's wallet. Standard terms:

Loan-to-Value: 50–60% of appraised fair market value

Duration: 30, 60, or 90-day active term

Monthly fee: 3.5–5% per month on the outstanding principal

Rollover option: full or partial extension without new appraisal (Module 3)

Repayment triggers cNFT burn and physical asset release

The 50–60% LTV is the key risk parameter. At 50% LTV, the collateral must lose more than 50% of its appraised value before the protocol faces any loss — a threshold that luxury watches, gold, and fine jewelry virtually never breach in the 30–90 day timeframe of a single loan cycle.

1.3 The Vault-Loan Matching Engine
Vault deposits are matched to active loan requests by the protocol's smart contract. Priority matching flows:

New borrower request submitted and validated on-chain

Protocol draws from the appropriate duration vault

Funds disbursed to borrower in under one minute

Borrower's cNFT is locked in the protocol's escrow account

Interest accrues per block; vault NAV updates in real time

This is not a peer-to-peer matching model (i.e., one lender to one borrower). It is a pooled model — all lenders in the 90-day vault collectively fund all 90-day loans. This is the approach used by Goldfinch's Senior Pool, Maple's lending pools, and Centrifuge's DROP/TIN structure — all industry-validated architectures.

SECTION 2: THE EARLY EXIT PROBLEM — LENDER LIQUIDITY BEFORE LOAN MATURITY
This is one of the most frequently under-solved problems in real-world asset lending protocols, and Vaulx must address it explicitly. Goldfinch's analysis confirmed: "Withdrawing liquidity from Goldfinch pools is dependent on the amount of liquidity available in the pools, meaning lenders may not be able to access liquidity when they require it." A lender who wants out before the 90-day loan matures has nowhere to go on most protocols.

Vaulx's solution is a three-layer liquidity architecture:

Layer 1 — Liquidity Reserve Buffer (10% of vault):
A mandatory 10% cash reserve is maintained in each vault at all times — never deployed to loans. This services early withdrawal requests up to that threshold without touching active loan positions. Any withdrawal request under 10% of the vault's total size is processed instantly on-chain. This is the same mechanism used by money market funds to handle normal redemption traffic.

Layer 2 — Vault Position Secondary Market:
Lenders who wish to exit beyond the 10% reserve threshold can list their vault share tokens (vGFI) on Vaulx's internal secondary market. Another user purchases the shares at the prevailing NAV (discounted by a secondary market spread of 0.5–2% to compensate the buyer for taking on duration risk). The exiting lender receives stablecoins immediately; the buyer inherits the yield position. This is the mechanism Goldfinch attempted via FIDU-USDC Curve pool — Vaulx builds it natively as a first-class product feature.

Layer 3 — Tranching (for larger vaults):
As vault TVL scales beyond R$5 million, Vaulx implements a senior/junior tranche structure (Centrifuge's DROP/TIN model). Senior tranche lenders receive first-priority repayment but lower yield (8–10% APY). Junior tranche backers accept first-loss exposure and earn higher yield (14–18% APY). Backers can withdraw as loan repayments come in. This structure aligns incentives and provides a capital buffer that protects senior depositors.

The combination of these three layers means Vaulx lenders are never locked with no exit. The secondary market and tranche structure are not complex to implement on Solana — they are standard DeFi vault patterns that Edson can build from established Anchor framework blueprints.

SECTION 3: DEFAULT AND RECOVERY — WHAT HAPPENS WHEN BORROWERS DON'T REPAY
Default mechanics are Vaulx's highest-stakes design decision. The protocol must be seen to make lenders whole or the product fails. The evidence supports a very strong recovery position.

3.1 Industry Baseline: Pawn Loan Repayment Rates
The industry benchmark from the National Pawnbrokers Association: 80–85% of pawn loans are repaid and collateral is redeemed. This is a structural characteristic of pawn lending, not Vaulx-specific optimism. Borrowers repay because they want their items back — a Rolex Submariner, a diamond necklace, a signed watch — items with personal value, not just financial value.

At Vaulx's 50–60% LTV, the borrower is always in the money to repay. If a borrower pledged an asset worth R$20,000 and received a R$10,000 loan, they have R$10,000 of equity sitting in the custody facility. This is the strongest repayment incentive structure in any credit product.

3.2 When Default Occurs: The Recovery Waterfall
For the 15–20% of loans that default, Vaulx's recovery process follows a defined waterfall:

Step 1 — Grace Period and Notification (Days 1–10 post-maturity):
The smart contract detects non-repayment at loan maturity. An automated notification is sent to the borrower's wallet and registered contact. A 10-day grace period is offered with a penalty surcharge (2% of principal). Many borrowers who "default" are simply late — this grace period captures most of them without triggering liquidation.

Step 2 — Auction on Vaulx Platform (Days 11–40):
If the grace period expires unpaid, Vaulx lists the physical asset for auction on its native platform. High-resolution photography, appraisal documentation, and provenance records are already on-chain (IPFS-linked via the cNFT). Bidders can purchase the asset directly. Reserve price is set at 110% of outstanding loan balance (principal + accrued interest + fees). If the auction price exceeds the reserve, the surplus is returned to the borrower's wallet.

This is the established pawn industry practice and the most operationally clean recovery route. Pre-owned luxury goods sold at auction with documented provenance move quickly. Chrono24 operates with 9 million monthly users and 560,000 listed watches; the secondary watch market showed +8.43% average transaction price growth in Q4 2025 YoY. Jewelry represents 70% of pawn shop inventory value, confirming deep secondary market liquidity for Vaulx's primary collateral categories.

Step 3 — NFT Ownership Transfer (Alternative to Auction):
A structurally more elegant option for high-value assets: instead of holding a physical auction, Vaulx mints a full ownership NFT representing title to the physical asset and sells it on secondary NFT markets or via direct OTC sale to luxury asset buyers. The buyer receives the ownership NFT; upon completion of KYC and payment, the physical asset is transferred from Vaulx's custody facility to the buyer's designated address. This is a faster and potentially higher-recovery-rate alternative for assets valued above R$50,000 where the pool of qualified buyers is smaller but more motivated.

Step 4 — Fractional NFT Recovery (for very high-value single assets):
For assets valued above R$200,000 — a high-end watch, a piece of fine art, a classic vehicle — Vaulx can fractionalize ownership. The asset's cNFT is upgraded to a fractional ownership contract; 100–1,000 shares are minted and sold to a pool of buyers. Each share holder owns a proportional percentage of the physical asset. This is the same mechanism used in art tokenization platforms (Masterworks, for example, fractionalized Basquiat and Warhol works). The protocol recovers 100% of the loan principal rapidly by distributing the asset's value across many buyers rather than finding a single buyer for a high-ticket item.

Step 5 — Insurance Layer (Phase 2):
Vaulx partners with specialized physical asset insurance providers (e.g., Lloyds of London syndicates, Brazilian specialist insurers such as Mapfre Seguros) to insure custody against theft, damage, or natural disaster. Separately, a protocol-level insurance fund — seeded with 1% of origination fees — provides a first-loss buffer for lenders in the event that auction recovery falls below outstanding loan principal. At 50–60% LTV, this scenario requires a collateral price collapse of over 40%, which is exceptionally rare for the target asset classes within 30–90 day windows.

3.3 Expected Recovery Rate at 50–60% LTV
Based on EIB data, recovery rates for private collateralized lending average 72.2%, with over 50% of defaulted contracts achieving recovery rates above 90%. At Vaulx's conservative 50–60% LTV, the structural recovery rate should be significantly higher — the protocol needs only to liquidate the asset above 50% of its appraised value to achieve full principal recovery. For liquid secondary market assets (watches, jewelry), this is achievable in virtually all non-catastrophic market conditions.

Target protocol recovery rate on defaulted loans: >90% of outstanding principal.

SECTION 4: REVENUE MODEL — ALL STREAMS
Vaulx's revenue architecture has five streams across two phases.

Phase 1 Streams (Operational at Launch)
Stream 1 — Origination Fee (borrower):
2–3% of loan principal charged at disbursement. On a R$10,000 loan, this is R$200–300 collected immediately at origination, before any interest accrues. This is the protocol's highest-certainty revenue — it is earned the moment a loan is disbursed, regardless of whether the loan is repaid or rolled over.

At 400 loans per month averaging R$10,000 principal: origination fee revenue = R$80,000–120,000/month. At average volume scale (600 loans/month, R$15,000 average): R$180,000–270,000/month.

Stream 2 — Interest Rate Spread (lender/borrower spread):
Borrowers pay 3.5–5% per month. Lenders earn 0.8–1.2% per month (10–14% APY). The spread of approximately 2.5–3.5% per month on deployed capital is Vaulx's margin. On R$4 million in outstanding loans, the monthly spread revenue is R$100,000–140,000.

Stream 3 — Rollover Fee:
A borrower who extends a loan for a second 30-day cycle pays an additional flat fee of 1.5–2% of principal in addition to the normal monthly interest. This is a pure margin improvement: no new appraisal cost, no new intake cost, no custody setup cost. The rollover is 100% incremental revenue. At a 60% rollover rate (industry-supported; pawn data shows average 2.15 loan extensions per loan), rollover fees add a significant recurring revenue layer.

Stream 4 — Liquidation Processing Fee:
When a default reaches auction or NFT sale, Vaulx charges a 5–8% liquidation processing fee on the sale proceeds. This aligns the protocol's incentives with efficient recovery — the higher the recovery, the higher the fee.

Stream 5 — Physical Asset Storage Fee (custody rent):
Borrowers pay a nominal weekly or monthly storage and insurance contribution for their item in custody — R$15–50 per item depending on size and value. At 2,000 active custodied items: R$30,000–100,000/month in recurring custody revenue, with zero incremental cost as Marcelo and Rodrigo's existing Gitel infrastructure absorbs this at near-zero marginal cost.

Phase 2 Streams (Post-Product-Market-Fit)
Stream 6 — VaaS (Vault as a Service):
White-label the Vaulx vault and loan smart contracts to third-party physical asset lenders — jewelry chains, auction houses, luxury retailers — who want to offer on-chain collateral lending without building the infrastructure. Vaulx charges a platform fee (0.5–1% of loan origination) plus a monthly SaaS license (R$2,000–10,000/month per partner). Five enterprise partners generate R$10,000–50,000/month in recurring SaaS revenue.

Stream 7 — Asset Scoring API:
Vaulx's appraisal database — built through every loan originated — becomes a proprietary luxury asset pricing oracle. Third parties (insurance companies, estate planners, tax authorities, lenders) pay for API access to real-time luxury asset valuations. Priced at R$500–5,000/month per subscriber. This is a flywheel data asset: every loan enriches the model; the richer the model, the more valuable the API.

Stream 8 — On-Chain Securitization (institutional):
As loan volume scales, Vaulx pools CCBs (Cédulas de Crédito Bancário) into a FIDC-equivalent tokenized structure and sells tranched positions to institutional investors. Senior tranche yield: 12–15% in BRL terms. This is the playbook Centrifuge used to grow to $289 million in active loans. It converts Vaulx from a direct lender to a marketplace and asset manager — materially higher valuation multiple.

SECTION 6: THE MOAT — WHY THIS CANNOT BE REPLICATED IN 18 MONTHS
The moat analysis is the most important section for Colosseum judges and future investors. Vaulx's defensibility is multi-layered and cannot be replicated by throwing capital at the problem.

Moat Layer 1 — The Custody Network (Operational, Not Capital)
Physical collateral lending requires a custody network of verified intake facilities, trained appraisers, secure storage, transport protocols, and insurance coverage across Brazil's major cities. Gitel provides this network from day one.

A competitor with R$10 million in funding could not replicate Gitel's custody network in 18 months. Building a compliant, Federal-Police-regulated physical security operation in Brazil requires: licensing, facility buildout, staff hiring and training, insurance procurement, and established bank relationships. Gitel has spent over a decade building exactly this. The custody layer is not a technology problem — it is an operational accumulation problem. This is the category of moat that Warren Buffett calls a "distribution moat": not a patent, not an algorithm, but an operational network that requires time and relationships to build, regardless of capital.

Moat Layer 2 — Appraisal Data and Price Oracle
Every loan Vaulx originates contributes a data point to the proprietary luxury asset pricing database: what was appraised, at what value, what happened to the secondary market price 90 days later, whether it was redeemed or liquidated and at what price. After 1,000 loans, this database has no public substitute. After 10,000 loans, it is a defensible pricing oracle for the Brazilian luxury market.

Chrono24's data shows the secondary watch market is increasingly driven by price index transparency — the ChronoPulse index is described as "an actual watch exchange" based on hundreds of thousands of real transactions. Vaulx's domestic equivalent, built from Brazilian luxury asset transactions, would be the only source of that data in the Brazilian market. This is a data moat that compounds with every origination.

Moat Layer 3 — On-Chain Credit History
Vaulx's cNFT minting system creates an on-chain record for every borrower: asset submitted, appraised value, loan terms, repayment or default outcome. After a borrower completes three cycles, their on-chain credit record is a verifiable asset. This is the "trust through consensus" mechanism that Goldfinch and Credix use to underwrite repeat borrowers at lower cost.

A borrower with a four-cycle Vaulx history can access preferential rates (lower fee, higher LTV) on subsequent loans. This creates lock-in: switching to a competitor requires rebuilding a credit history from zero. The borrower's on-chain reputation is anchored to Vaulx's protocol.

Moat Layer 4 — Regulatory Positioning
Brazil's SCD license framework, under BCB Resolution 5,050, allows Vaulx to operate as a direct credit society — issuing CCBs without the R$1 million+ capital requirement of a full banking license. The regulatory window for fintech lending in Brazil has been defined; a competitor starting the licensing process today faces a 12–18 month regulatory runway before they can legally originate loans.

Brazil's crypto asset regulatory framework (BCB Resolutions 519, 520, 521, published November 2025) creates a compliance requirement for SPSAV authorization. Vaulx's early compliance positioning — building to the new framework from inception — is a first-mover regulatory advantage. Late entrants will face a more congested authorization queue and higher compliance scrutiny.

Moat Layer 5 — Network Effects (Lender Side)
A vault with R$10 million in deposits can fund borrowers on better terms (lower fees, faster disbursement, higher LTV flexibility) than a vault with R$1 million. As Vaulx's lender TVL grows, it can offer structurally superior borrower terms, which attracts more borrowers, which generates more yield, which attracts more lenders. This is the classic two-sided marketplace compounding effect. The custody moat is what makes entry into this flywheel prohibitively difficult — a new entrant cannot compete on terms without the custody layer, and the custody layer takes years to build.

SECTION 7: THE SOLANA STRATEGIC ALIGNMENT
Vaulx does not merely happen to run on Solana. The Solana choice is structurally integral to the economics and the strategic positioning.

Sub-cent transaction fees (<$0.001) make Vaulx's R$10,000–50,000 loan sizes economically viable. On Ethereum, gas costs alone would consume 2–5% of loan value on each origination.

Compressed NFTs (Metaplex Bubblegum) allow cNFT minting at near-zero cost — each custody proof costs less than $0.001. Ethereum NFT minting would cost $5–50 per item.

Solana's RWA ecosystem is growing rapidly — RWA lending deposits exceeded $1.2 billion in March 2026, with Solana leading all chains in RWA lending share. Every institutional participant building in Solana's RWA space is a potential partner, liquidity provider, or acquirer.

Vaulx as a physical RWA protocol fills the single most prominent gap in Solana's DeFi ecosystem: movable physical collateral. Every other RWA protocol on Solana uses financial instruments. Vaulx's existence makes Solana's RWA narrative more complete — this is why Solana ecosystem funds and grants programs are natural early supporters.

The pitch to Colosseum is therefore not just "invest in Vaulx" but "Vaulx makes Solana the home of physical RWA globally." That is a strategic argument that resonates with ecosystem-focused grant programs.
